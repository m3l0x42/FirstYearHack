Education: Health Exercises

Conditions

Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

A leading cause of vision loss in older adults; damages the macula and affects sharp central vision.

Cataracts

Clouding of the natural lens of the eye, causing blurry or dim vision; extremely common with aging.

Glaucoma

A group of diseases that damage the optic nerve—often due to high eye pressure—leading to gradual, irreversible vision loss.

Diabetic Retinopathy

Vision-threatening damage to the retinal blood vessels caused by diabetes.

Dry Eye

A chronic condition in which the eyes do not make enough tears or tears evaporate too quickly, causing irritation and blurred vision.

Retinal Detachment

A medical emergency where the retina separates from the back of the eye, risking permanent vision loss.

Pink Eye (Conjunctivitis)

Inflammation of the tissue covering the white of the eye, often due to infection or allergies; causes redness and discharge.

Amblyopia (Lazy Eye)

A childhood condition where one eye does not develop normal vision because the brain favors the other eye.

Macular Edema

Swelling in the macula due to fluid leakage, commonly from diabetes or vein occlusion; affects sharp central vision.

Floaters & Vitreous Detachment

Specks or strands in vision caused by changes in the gel inside the eye; vitreous detachment occurs when that gel pulls away from the retina.

Color Blindness

Difficulty distinguishing certain colors, usually due to genetic differences in retina cone cells.

Astigmatism

Irregular curvature of the cornea or lens causing blurred or distorted vision at all distances.

Nearsightedness (Myopia)

A refractive error where distance vision is blurry because the eye is too long or the cornea too curved.

Farsightedness (Hyperopia)

A refractive error where close objects are blurry because the eye is too short or the cornea too flat.

Exercise 2: Desk Stretches

Perform simple stretches at your desk to prevent muscle stiffness.

  • Neck Tilts: Gently tilt your head from side to side.
  • Shoulder Rolls: Roll your shoulders backwards and forwards.
  • Torso Twists: Gently twist your upper body from side to side.
Exercises

Short visual exercises to reduce eye strain and train coordination. Try a few throughout the day and stop any exercise if it causes discomfort.

Near-Far Focus

Hold a finger 10 inches from your eyes. Focus on it for 10 seconds, then look at something 20+ feet away for 10 seconds.

10-10-10 Method

Every 10 minutes, look about 10 feet away for at least 10 seconds to give your eyes a short rest.

Focus Shifting

Move a small object (like a pen) slowly forward and back while keeping it in focus for 2–3 minutes.

Tracking a Moving Object

Move your finger horizontally, vertically, and diagonally while following it with your eyes only. Do 3 sets of 30 seconds.

Zooming Exercise

Extend your arm with your thumb up. Bring the thumb slowly toward your nose while keeping focus. Repeat 10–15 times.

Eye Clock Exercise

Imagine a clock and look at each “number”: 12, 3, 6, 9, then the diagonals. Repeat 3 rounds.

Full Eye Circles

Move your eyes in a slow circle clockwise for 10 rotations, then counterclockwise. Repeat 2–3 times.

Up-Down Scanning

Look as far up as you can, then as far down. Repeat 10–15 times.

Side-to-Side Scanning

Look left as far as possible, then right. Repeat 10–15 times.

Diagonal Scanning

Look top-left → bottom-right, then top-right → bottom-left. Repeat 10–15 times.

Palming

Rub your hands until warm. Cup them gently over your eyes without pressure for 2–3 minutes.

Blinking Drill

Blink rapidly for 10 seconds, then close your eyes gently for 10 seconds. Repeat 5 times.

Sunning (Indoor or Outdoor)

Close eyes and let warm light pass through the eyelids. Move your head side to side slowly for 1–2 minutes.

Deep Eye Relaxation

Close eyes, take slow deep breaths, and consciously relax eye muscles for about 2 minutes.

Dark Room Rest

Sit in a dim or dark room with eyes closed for 3-5 minutes to rest and reset visual stimulation.

Pencil Push-Ups

Hold a pencil at arm's length and slowly bring it toward your nose while keeping it single and clear. Stop when it doubles. Repeat 10 times.

Brock String Exercise

Use a string with beads to train convergence and eye-team coordination (classic binocular therapy tool).

Near Point of Convergence Training

Focus on a small target moving toward your nose and try to maintain a single image. Repeat 5-10 times.

Stereo Vision Cards

Use depth-perception cards or apps to train both eyes to work together for improved stereo vision.

Fusion Training

Alternate focus between a near target and a far target to strengthen binocular coordination and fusion.

20–20–20 Rule (Essential)

Every 20 minutes, look 20 feet away for 20 seconds — a simple, effective habit to reduce digital eye strain.

Screen Blink Reminders

Consciously blink fully every 10 seconds for a minute to refresh the tear film and reduce dryness.

Micro-Breaks

Take 5-second breaks every 5 minutes to relax eye posture and refocus briefly.

Screen Distance Variation

Move your screen slightly forward or back every hour to re-challenge focusing and reduce adaptation strain.

The “Awareness Circle”

Look ahead and try to notice objects at the edges of your vision without moving your eyes to build peripheral awareness.

Side Awareness Training

Hold your hands out to your sides and wiggle your fingers. Try to see the movement with peripheral vision.

Moving Light Exercise

In a dim room, move a small light source side-to-side and follow it with your eyes to train smooth pursuit.
